# How to Conduct Yourself During a Technical Interview

This guide is designed to provide you with knowledge on how to communicate, structure your answers, and approach problems during a technical interview.

Technical interviews typically evaluate three things at once: your ability to communicate clearly, your conceptual understanding, and your problem-solving process. The advice below is organized around those goals.

---

## 1. How to Structure a Response to a Technical Question

This applies to conceptual or knowledge-based questions — for example, "What's the difference between a stack and a queue?" or "How does hashing work?"

- **Lead with a direct answer.** Don't bury your main point in a long windup. Answer the question first, then elaborate.
- **Follow with supporting detail.** Explain the "why" behind your answer — the mechanics, reasoning, or definition that backs it up.
- **Use a concrete example or analogy.** Demonstrating a concept with a simple example shows real understanding, not just memorization.
- **Mention trade-offs or related concepts when relevant.** For example, if asked about arrays vs. linked lists, briefly noting when you'd use one over the other shows deeper understanding.
- **Check in with the interviewer.** A brief "Does that answer what you were asking?" or "Would you like me to go deeper on any part of that?" keeps the conversation collaborative and gives you a chance to course-correct if you misunderstood the question.

## 2. Problem Solving Question Framework

This applies to coding or design problems, where the interviewer wants to see your process as much as your final answer.

1. **Understand the problem.** Listen fully before reacting, then restate the problem in your own words to confirm you understood it correctly.
2. **Ask clarifying questions.** Ask about constraints, expected input size, edge cases (empty input, duplicates, negative numbers), and whether you should optimize for time or space.
3. **Think out loud.** Narrate your reasoning as you consider different approaches — this is often the most important part of the interview for the interviewer.
4. **Propose an approach before coding.** Briefly explain your plan and mention trade-offs (e.g., time/space complexity, simpler vs. more optimal solutions) before writing any code.
5. **Implement while continuing to narrate.** Explain key decisions as you write code, rather than going silent.
6. **Test your solution.** Walk through your code with a sample input, and consider at least one edge case.
7. **Reflect if asked.** If prompted, discuss the time/space complexity of your solution or how you might improve it.

## 3. Other Tips

- **Handling questions you don't know the answer to:** Be honest rather than guessing confidently. Reason through what you do know out loud, and don't hesitate to ask for a hint — interviewers generally expect this and it's not a red flag.
- **Recovering from mistakes:** Bugs and errors are normal and expected. If you catch one, calmly explain what went wrong and how you're fixing it, rather than getting flustered or apologizing excessively.
- **Balancing silence and narration:** It's fine to think quietly for a short stretch while working through a hard part of a problem, but check in so the interviewer isn't left wondering what you're doing. "Sorry, I am just taking a moment to consider my response".
- **Managing nerves and pacing:** It's okay to pause and take a breath before answering a question rather than rushing into a response. A brief, composed pause often reads better than a fast but disorganized answer.

---

*Clear communication and a steady, structured approach often matter as much as arriving at a perfect answer.*
